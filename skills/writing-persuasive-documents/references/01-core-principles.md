# Core Principles

Professional writing fails for one reason more than any other: it tries to show what the writer knows instead of changing what the reader thinks. This guide covers the foundational principles for fixing that — whether the document is a report, proposal, memo, article, brief, or any document meant to influence a decision.

## The Core Shift

Readers pick up a document because they think it might be valuable *to them*. If it isn't, they stop — often within two paragraphs.

> **Stop writing to show what you know. Start writing to change what your readers think.**

Everything in this playbook follows from that single idea.

---

## 1. Value Is Everything

Clarity, organization, and persuasion all matter — but only after value is established. If your document is clear and useless, it's useless. If it's well-organized and useless, it's useless. If it's persuasive and useless, it's useless.

Value is not in the document. Value is in your readers. The same memo can be essential to one team and irrelevant to another. Value is what a specific set of readers gets out of reading your work.

### Before and After

**Before (writer-centered):**

> This report presents findings from a six-month analysis of customer onboarding workflows. We examined 14 process variants across three regions and catalogued 47 distinct friction points.

*Problem: The writer is showing what they did. The reader has no reason to care.*

**After (reader-centered):**

> Customer onboarding currently takes 23 days on average — but competitors have reduced theirs to 9. This gap is costing us an estimated $2.1M annually in churn during the first 90 days. The following analysis identifies the three workflow bottlenecks responsible for 80% of that delay, along with fixes that can be implemented this quarter.

*The reader immediately knows: there's a problem I care about, it has a measurable cost, and this document offers a solution.*

### Quick Test

After writing your opening, ask: *If I were my busiest, most skeptical reader, would I keep reading after this paragraph — or would I close the tab?* If you aren't sure, the opening isn't valuable enough yet.

## 2. Know Your Specific Readers

"Write for your audience" is common advice. It's also too vague to be useful. You need to know your readers with granularity:

- **What do they already believe** about your topic?
- **What do they want to understand** or what problem do they want solved?
- **What would they doubt** about your claims?
- **What language signals value** in their world?

Every professional community — legal, medical, engineering, financial, academic, policy — has its own coded language for signaling that something matters. Words like *risk*, *exposure*, *compliance gap* in legal contexts; *yield*, *margin compression*, *runway* in finance; *anomaly*, *inconsistent with*, *contrary to predictions* in research.

These codes are not jargon for jargon's sake — they are the words your readers use to decide whether your work is worth their time.

### Identifying the Codes

Identify the value-signaling terms of the reader's professional community by studying documents that successfully influenced decisions in the field — funded proposals, approved recommendations, memos that changed something. Look for recurring words and phrases that signal value, importance, urgency, or stakes.

### Before and After

**Before (generic):**

> We recommend updating the authentication system. The current system is old and there are some issues with it.

**After (coded for a security-conscious engineering audience):**

> The current authentication system has three known vulnerabilities that are actively exploited in the wild (CVE-2025-1142, CVE-2025-1307, CVE-2025-1419). Each represents an unmitigated attack vector to production customer data. We recommend migrating to OAuth 2.1 with PKCE, which eliminates all three vectors.

*"Unmitigated attack vector," "actively exploited in the wild," and specific CVE references are the codes that tell a security-minded reader: this matters right now.*

## 3. Open With a Problem They Care About

Most professional documents open with background, definitions, or history. This feels safe and logical — lay the foundation, then build. But readers don't process documents as construction projects. They process them as triage: *Is this worth my time?*

Background tells readers what's stable. But readers look for what's *unstable* — because instability is where value lives. If everything is fine, there's nothing to read about.

The structure that works:

1. **Shared context** — a sentence or two establishing common ground (not a paragraph of history)
2. **The problem** — something is wrong, inconsistent, at risk, or misunderstood
3. **The stakes** — what this problem costs or what solving it would yield
4. **Your response** — what this document will do about it

### Before and After

**Before (background-first):**

> Cloud computing has transformed how organizations manage infrastructure. Since the early 2010s, adoption rates have grown steadily. Our organization began its cloud migration in 2019. This report reviews the current state of our cloud infrastructure.

*Four sentences of stable background. No reason to keep reading.*

**After (problem-first):**

> Our cloud infrastructure costs increased 40% year-over-year while utilization dropped to 31%. We are paying for three times the capacity we use. This report identifies the four largest sources of waste and proposes consolidation changes projected to save $1.8M annually without affecting uptime.

*Sentence one is the problem. Sentence two is the cost. Sentence three is the response. Background is absent because the reader doesn't need it — they already know what cloud infrastructure is.*

### The Anti-Pattern: The "Martini Glass"

A widespread but counterproductive structure opens with a broad generalization, narrows to a specific thesis, discusses it, then broadens back out to a generalization at the end. This structure signals to the reader that the writer is *easing into* the topic — which tells them nothing is urgent. Invert it. Lead with the specific, urgent problem. Broaden later if needed.

## 4. Use the Language of Tension

Words like *and*, *also*, *moreover*, *in addition*, and *furthermore* signal continuity and stability. They tell the reader: everything is fine, here's more of the same.

Words like *but*, *however*, *although*, *yet*, *inconsistent*, *contrary to*, *despite*, and *nevertheless* signal tension and instability. They tell the reader: something is wrong — and what follows might matter to you.

This is not about "transition words" or "flow." It's about function. Stability language tells readers there's no problem. Tension language tells readers there *is* one. Since readers are scanning for problems worth their attention, tension language is what earns you their continued reading.

### Before and After

**Before (stability language):**

> The team completed all deliverables on schedule. In addition, we implemented the new reporting dashboard. Furthermore, the QA process was expanded to include regression testing. Also, we onboarded two new team members.

*The reader's internal response: "Great. No problems. I can stop reading."*

**After (tension language):**

> The team completed all deliverables on schedule. However, three of the five deliverables required last-minute scope reductions to meet the deadline, and two critical features were deferred to next quarter. Although the new reporting dashboard is live, it currently lacks the filtering capability that stakeholders identified as their top priority.

*Same project, same facts — but now the reader sees instability and keeps reading to find out what it means.*

### A Crucial Nuance

Tension language is not negativity. You are not complaining. You are showing your readers that something they care about is not as settled as they might assume — and that your document addresses it. This is how value is created.

## 5. Show the Cost of Inaction

Identifying an instability is necessary but not sufficient. Readers also need to know: *Does this instability actually matter?* Many things are imperfect. Most imperfections don't warrant action.

You need to show either:

- **Cost** — what the problem is costing them right now (money, time, risk, credibility, competitive position), or
- **Benefit** — what solving the problem would yield (revenue, efficiency, risk reduction, strategic advantage)

Cost and benefit are different rhetorical moves with different tones. Some reader communities respond more to cost language ("we are losing..."), others to benefit language ("we could gain..."). Pay attention to which framing dominates in successful documents within your field, and follow that pattern.

### Before and After

**Before (instability without stakes):**

> Our employee retention rate has declined from 89% to 74% over the past two years.

*The reader thinks: "That's not great, but is it actually a problem for us?"*

**After (instability with cost):**

> Our employee retention rate has declined from 89% to 74% over the past two years. At current replacement costs of $45K per departing employee, this represents an additional $2.7M in annual hiring spend — and exits are concentrated among senior engineers, where institutional knowledge loss compounds the direct cost.

**After (instability with benefit):**

> Our employee retention rate has declined from 89% to 74% over the past two years. Returning to 85% — achievable with the targeted interventions in this proposal — would save approximately $1.9M annually and preserve the senior engineering expertise that currently takes 18 months to rebuild after each departure.

*Both versions give the reader a reason to care. The first emphasizes what they're losing. The second emphasizes what they could gain.*
