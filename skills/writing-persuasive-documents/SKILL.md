# Writing Persuasive Documents

Write to change what readers think — not to show what you know.

## Workflow: Writing a new document

1. **Identify specific readers.** Determine what they already believe, what they want solved, what they would doubt, and what language signals value in their world.
2. **Identify the core instability.** Find what is wrong, at risk, or misunderstood — instability is where value lives.
3. **Open with problem + stakes + response.** State the problem within 3 sentences. Show the cost or benefit. State what the document will do about it. No background preamble.
4. **Use tension language.** Lead with *but, however, although, despite, yet, inconsistent, contrary to*. Avoid stability language (*also, furthermore, in addition, moreover*) in the opening.
5. **Show cost of inaction or benefit of action.** Quantify with money, time, risk, or competitive position. Match the framing (cost vs. benefit) to what resonates in the reader's community.
6. **Argue, don't explain.** Anticipate what the reader will doubt and address each doubt directly. Do not narrate your research process.
7. **Address the strongest counterargument.** Acknowledge it, then show why it doesn't hold. This builds trust.
8. **Challenge what's wrong, not what's missing.** "The current approach produces the wrong result" is stronger than "nobody has studied X."
9. **Build evidence toward the claim.** Connect sources to create tension and instability — do not catalogue them neutrally.
10. **Restructure for the reader.** Put the most important claim first. Follow the reader's decision process, not your discovery chronology. Cut everything that doesn't serve the argument.
11. **Self-review.** Check against the revision checklist in [references/02-advanced-techniques.md](references/02-advanced-techniques.md).

## Workflow: Reviewing an existing draft

1. Read the full document.
2. Check against the revision checklist in [references/02-advanced-techniques.md](references/02-advanced-techniques.md).
3. Identify the 3 highest-impact violations.
4. For each issue, provide a concrete before/after rewrite demonstrating the fix.
5. Name which principle each fix addresses (see quick reference below).

## Quick reference

| Principle | Instead of... | Do this... |
|---|---|---|
| Value first | Showing what you know | Changing what they think |
| Specific readers | Writing for "the audience" | Writing for the 3 people who decide |
| Problem-first openings | Background, definitions, history | The instability + its cost, immediately |
| Tension language | Also, furthermore, in addition | But, however, although, despite |
| Cost of inaction | "There is a gap" | "This is costing us $X / exposing us to Y" |
| Argue, don't explain | "We analyzed X and found Y" | "The obvious objection is Z — here's why it doesn't hold" |
| Challenge, don't fill | "Nobody has studied X" | "The current approach to X is producing the wrong result" |
| Restructure for readers | Draft organized by your discovery process | Document organized by their decision process |

## Principle selection

Not every document needs all principles equally. Adjust intensity to context:

- **Proposals / recommendations / business cases**: Apply all principles at full intensity.
- **Executive summaries**: Prioritize problem-first opening, cost of inaction, tension language.
- **Status reports**: Apply tension language and cost framing where genuine issues exist. Do not manufacture drama.
- **Technical briefs**: Prioritize argue-don't-explain and evidence building.
- **Emails requesting action**: Prioritize value first, problem opening, cost of inaction. Keep short.
